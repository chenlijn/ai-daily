📄 Top 10 arXiv Papers (March 10, 2026)

Time window: March 9, 8:00 AM - March 10, 8:00 AM (Beijing Time)

1. Agentic Critical Training (ACT)
arXiv:2603.08706 | Weize Liu et al. | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08706
Problem: Traditional imitation learning for LLM agents teaches what to do but not why - agents lack awareness of action quality because they never contrast successful actions against suboptimal alternatives.
Method: ACT introduces a reinforcement learning paradigm that trains agents to identify better actions among alternatives by rewarding correct judgment. The model develops genuine self-reflection capabilities rather than imitating pre-constructed reflection text.
Results: ACT achieves average improvements of 5.07 points over imitation learning and 4.62 points over standard RL. Compared to knowledge distillation approaches, ACT yields 2.42 points improvement with strong out-of-distribution generalization.
Conclusion: ACT provides a promising path toward developing more reflective and capable LLM agents by enabling genuine autonomous reasoning about action quality.
2. RetroAgent: From Solving to Evolving via Retrospective Dual Intrinsic Feedback
arXiv:2603.08561 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08561
Problem: Standard RL paradigms favor static problem-solving over continuous adaptation; agents converge to suboptimal strategies due to insufficient exploration.
Method: RetroAgent features hindsight self-reflection producing dual intrinsic feedback: (1) numerical feedback tracking subtask completion, and (2) language feedback distilling lessons into memory. Uses SimUtil-UCB strategy balancing relevance, utility, and exploration.
Results: Surpasses GRPO by +18.3% on ALFWorld, +15.4% on WebShop, +27.1% on Sokoban, +8.9% on MineSweeper. Strong test-time adaptation and OOD generalization.
Conclusion: Retrospective self-reflection enables agent evolution beyond static problem-solving with strong test-time adaptation.
3. CoCo: Code as CoT for Text-to-Image Preview and Rare Concept Generation
arXiv:2603.08652 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08652
Problem: Existing CoT-based text-to-image methods rely on abstract natural-language planning, lacking precision for complex spatial layouts, structured visual elements, and dense textual content.
Method: CoCo (Code-as-CoT) uses executable code to represent reasoning process. Generates code specifying scene layout, executes in sandboxed environment to render deterministic draft image, then refines through fine-grained editing. Built CoCo-10K dataset.
Results: Achieved improvements of +68.83% on StructT2IBench, +54.8% on OneIG-Bench, and +41.23% on LongText-Bench over direct generation. Outperformed other CoT-empowered generation methods.
Conclusion: Executable code is an effective and reliable reasoning paradigm for precise, controllable, and structured text-to-image generation. Code: https://github.com/micky-li-hd/CoCo
4. OfficeQA Pro: An Enterprise Benchmark for End-to-End Grounded Reasoning
arXiv:2603.08655 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08655
Problem: AI agents struggle with grounded, multi-document reasoning over large heterogeneous document corpora in enterprise settings.
Method: OfficeQA Pro uses U.S. Treasury Bulletins spanning nearly 100 years (89,000 pages, 26M+ numerical values) with 133 questions requiring precise document parsing, retrieval, and analytical reasoning. Evaluated Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini 3.1 Pro.
Results: Frontier LLMs achieved less than 5% accuracy on parametric knowledge alone and less than 12% with web access. With direct document corpus access: only 34.1% average. Structured document representation from ai_parse_document yielded 16.1% relative performance gain.
Conclusion: Significant headroom remains before agents can be considered reliable at enterprise-grade grounded reasoning. Structured document representation is critical.
5. FinToolBench: Evaluating LLM Agents for Real-World Financial Tool Use
arXiv:2603.08262 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08262
Problem: Financial AI evaluation focuses on static textual analysis, ignoring complex tool execution reality. General benchmarks lack domain-specific rigor.
Method: First real-world benchmark with 760 executable financial tools and 295 tool-required queries. Evaluation on timeliness, intent type, regulatory alignment.
Results: FATR baseline enhances stability and compliance through finance-aware retrieval and reasoning.
Conclusion: Sets new standard for trustworthy AI in finance with auditable agentic execution.
6. M3-ACE: Rectifying Visual Perception in Multimodal Math Reasoning
arXiv:2603.08369 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08369
Problem: Multimodal LLM performance limited by inaccurate visual perception. Most failures from incorrect visual evidence extraction, not reasoning deficiencies.
Method: Decouples perception and reasoning via shared context on visual evidence lists. Multi-agent collaboration exposes inconsistencies. Summary and Refine tools organize/filter evidence.
Results: New SOTA of 89.1 on MathVision with consistent improvements on MathVista and MathVerse.
Conclusion: Perception-centric multi-agent collaboration is essential for multimodal reasoning advancement.
7. Trust via Reputation of Conviction
arXiv:2603.08575 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08575
Problem: How to establish trust in AI systems when evaluating claims and sources - traditional approaches focus on correctness or faithfulness which may not be regime-independent.
Method: Formalizes knowledge, truth, and trust mathematically. Develops reputation framework grounded in "conviction" - the likelihood that a source's stance is vindicated by independent consensus.
Results: Conviction is regime-independent, rewards genuine contribution, and demands transparent self-sufficient perceptions enabling external verification.
Conclusion: For AI agents (capable but error-prone sources), verifiable conviction and continuously accrued reputation constitute the only robust foundation for trust.
8. IronEngine: Towards General AI Assistant
arXiv:2603.08425 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08425
Problem: Current AI assistants lack unified orchestration across desktop UI, APIs, clients, model backends, memory, task scheduling, skills, and tools.
Method: IronEngine features unified orchestration core with three-phase pipeline: Discussion (Planner-Reviewer), Model Switch (VRAM-aware), Execution (tool-augmented loop). Hierarchical memory with multi-level consolidation, vectorized skill repository (ChromaDB), adaptive model management (92 model profiles), intelligent tool routing with 130+ alias normalization.
Results: File operation benchmarks: 100% task completion with mean total time of 1541 seconds across four heterogeneous tasks.
Conclusion: Positions IronEngine as system-oriented foundation for general-purpose personal assistants, automation frameworks, and future human-centered agent platforms.
9. In-Context Reinforcement Learning for Tool Use in LLMs
arXiv:2603.08068 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08068
Problem: Enabling LLM tool use typically requires expensive SFT followed by RL with substantial labeled data.
Method: ICRL is RL-only framework eliminating SFT need via few-shot prompting during rollout. Gradual reduction to zero-shot tool calling.
Results: State-of-the-art performance across reasoning and tool-use benchmarks as scalable, data-efficient alternative.
Conclusion: Tool use can be learned purely through in-context RL without expensive supervised pre-training.
10. The Struggle Between Continuation and Refusal: Mechanistic Analysis of Jailbreak
arXiv:2603.08234 | Mon, 9 Mar 2026
https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08234
Problem: LLMs remain vulnerable to jailbreaking; root causes poorly understood at mechanistic level.
Method: Comprehensive mechanistic interpretability at attention head level. Causal interventions and activation scaling.
Results: Jailbreaks arise from competition between intrinsic continuation drive and safety defenses. Notable functional differences in safety heads across architectures.
Conclusion: Novel mechanistic perspective for understanding jailbreak behaviors with implications for safety improvements.
