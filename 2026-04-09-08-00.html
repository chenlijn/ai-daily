📄 Top 10 arXiv Papers

1. MegaTrain: Full Precision Training of 100B+ Parameter Large Language Models on a Single GPU
Authors: Zhengqing Yuan et al. | Subjects: cs.CL, cs.DC, cs.OS | arXiv:2604.05091
Problem: Training 100B+ parameter LLMs requires massive GPU memory for parameters and optimizer states, making it inaccessible to most researchers.
Method: MegaTrain stores parameters in host memory (CPU) and treats GPUs as transient compute engines. Uses pipelined double-buffered execution to overlap prefetching, computation, and gradient offloading. Replaces persistent autograd graphs with stateless layer templates.
Results: On a single H200 GPU with 1.5TB host memory, MegaTrain trains models up to 120B parameters. Achieves 1.84x training throughput vs DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 for 14B models. Enables 512k token context training on a single GH200.
Conclusion: Memory-centric design enables democratization of large model training without requiring expensive GPU clusters.
2. Claw-Eval: Toward Trustworthy Evaluation of Autonomous Agents
Authors: Bowen Ye, Rang Li et al. | Subjects: cs.AI | arXiv:2604.06132
Problem: Existing agent benchmarks suffer from trajectory-opaque grading, underspecified safety evaluation, and narrow modality coverage.
Method: Claw-Eval provides 300 human-verified tasks across 9 categories with trajectory-aware grading using three evidence channels (execution traces, audit logs, environment snapshots). Evaluates Completion, Safety, and Robustness with Pass@k metrics.
Results: Testing 14 frontier models reveals trajectory-opaque evaluation misses 44% of safety violations and 13% of robustness failures. Multimodal performance varies sharply—most models perform poorer on video than documents/images.
Conclusion: Provides actionable directions for building agents that are not only capable but reliably deployable in real-world environments.
3. In-Place Test-Time Training (ICLR 2026 Oral)
Authors: Shengjie Luo et al. | Subjects: cs.LG, cs.AI, cs.CL | arXiv:2604.06169
Problem: Traditional "train then deploy" paradigm limits LLMs from adapting to continuous streams of new information. Existing Test-Time Training methods face architectural incompatibility and misaligned objectives.
Method: In-Place TTT treats the final MLP projection matrix as adaptable fast weights, enabling drop-in enhancement for LLMs. Replaces generic reconstruction with a next-token-prediction-aligned objective and uses chunk-wise update mechanism for context parallelism.
Results: Enables a 4B model to achieve superior performance on tasks with contexts up to 128k tokens. When pretrained from scratch, consistently outperforms competitive TTT-related approaches.
Conclusion: Establishes In-Place TTT as a promising step towards continual learning in LLMs.
4. How LLMs Follow Instructions: Skillful Coordination, Not a Universal Mechanism
Authors: Elisabetta Rocchetti et al. | Subjects: cs.AI | arXiv:2604.06015
Problem: Instruction tuning is assumed to endow language models with domain-general instruction-following, but the underlying mechanism remains poorly understood.
Method: Diagnostic probing across nine diverse tasks in three instruction-tuned models. Analyzed cross-task transfer, causal ablation, layer-wise complexity, and temporal dynamics.
Results: Converging evidence against a universal mechanism: general probes underperform task-specific specialists, cross-task transfer is weak and clustered by skill similarity, and tasks stratify by complexity across layers.
Conclusion: Instruction-following is better characterized as skillful coordination of diverse linguistic capabilities rather than a single abstract constraint-checking process.
5. HybridKV: Hybrid KV Cache Compression for Efficient Multimodal LLM Inference
Authors: Bowen Zeng et al. | Subjects: cs.AI | arXiv:2604.05887
Problem: Multimodal LLMs suffer from rapid KV cache growth—each visual input expands into thousands of tokens, causing prohibitive memory overhead and latency.
Method: HybridKV integrates complementary compression strategies: classifies attention heads into static/dynamic types using text-centric attention, then applies top-down budget allocation with text-prior pruning for static heads and chunk-wise retrieval for dynamic heads.
Results: On Qwen2.5-VL-7B across 11 multimodal benchmarks, HybridKV reduces KV cache memory by up to 7.9x and achieves 1.52x faster decoding with minimal performance drop.
Conclusion: Heterogeneous attention head behaviors require distinct compression strategies for efficient MLLM inference.
6. On Dominant Manifolds in Reservoir Computing Networks
Authors: Noa Kaplan et al. | Subjects: cs.LG, math.DS, math.OC | arXiv:2604.05967
Problem: Understanding how training shapes the geometry of recurrent network dynamics is a central problem in time-series modeling with Reservoir Computing.
Method: For linear continuous-time reservoir models, links dimensionality and structure of dominant modes to intrinsic dimensionality of training data. Relates dominant modes to Koopman eigenfunction approximations.
Results: Illuminates explicit connection between reservoir computing and Dynamic Mode Decomposition. Demonstrates eigenvalue motion that generates dominant manifolds during training.
Conclusion: Provides theoretical foundation for understanding reservoir behavior and connection to established dynamical systems methods.
7. From Uniform to Learned Knots: Spline-Based Numerical Encodings for Tabular Deep Learning
Authors: Manish Kumar et al. | Subjects: cs.LG | arXiv:2604.05635
Problem: Role of explicit numerical preprocessing in tabular deep learning remains less understood despite its importance in classical ML.
Method: Studied three spline families (B-splines, M-splines, I-splines) with four knot-placement strategies (uniform, quantile, target-aware, learnable). Evaluated on regression/classification with MLP, ResNet, and FT-Transformer backbones.
Results: For classification, piecewise-linear encoding (PLE) is most robust; spline encodings remain competitive. For regression, no single encoding dominates—performance depends on spline family, knot strategy, and output size.
Conclusion: Numerical encodings should be assessed not only by predictive performance but also computational overhead.
8. Reproducing AlphaZero on Tablut: Self-Play RL for an Asymmetric Board Game
Authors: Tõnis Lees et al. | Subjects: cs.LG | arXiv:2604.05476
Problem: Original AlphaZero architecture uses single policy/value heads for symmetric games, but asymmetric games force the network to learn conflicting evaluation functions.
Method: Modified architecture uses separate policy/value heads per player role while maintaining shared residual trunk. Applied C4 data augmentation, increased replay buffer, and past checkpoint opponents (25% of games).
Results: Over 100 self-play iterations, achieved BayesElo rating of 1235 vs random baseline. Training showed decreased policy entropy and average remaining pieces.
Conclusion: AlphaZero framework can transfer to highly asymmetric games with proper architectural modifications and stabilization techniques.
9. Cross-fitted Proximal Learning for Model-Based Reinforcement Learning
Authors: Nishanth Venkatesh Senthil Kumar et al. | Subjects: cs.LG, eess.SY | arXiv:2604.05185
Problem: In offline settings with hidden confounding, models learned directly from observational data may be biased, especially in partially observable systems.
Method: Formulated bridge learning as conditional moment restriction problem with nuisance objects from conditional mean embedding and density. Developed K-fold cross-fitted extension of two-stage bridge estimator.
Results: Proposed procedure preserves original identification strategy while using data more efficiently than single sample split. Derived oracle-comparator bound with decomposed error terms.
Conclusion: Enables more efficient and reliable policy evaluation in confounded partially observable MDPs.
10. Towards Scaling Law Analysis for Spatiotemporal Weather Data
Authors: Alexander Kiefer et al. | Subjects: cs.LG | arXiv:2604.05068
Problem: Compute-optimal scaling laws are well-studied for NLP/CV but weather forecasting is harder: autoregressive rollouts compound errors, outputs couple many physical channels with disparate scales.
Method: Extended neural scaling analysis from single-step training loss to long rollouts and per-channel metrics. Quantified error distribution across channels and horizons, tested power law scaling for pooled vs per-channel metrics.
Results: Found strong cross-channel and cross-horizon heterogeneity. Pooled scaling can look favorable while many channels degrade at late leads.
Conclusion: Has implications for weighted objectives, horizon-aware curricula, and resource allocation across outputs in weather forecasting.
