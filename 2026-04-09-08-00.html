📄 Top 10 arXiv Papers

1. How Much LLM Does a Self-Revising Agent Actually Need?

arXiv: 2604.07236 2026-04-09

Problem: The paper asks how much of an agent's capability truly comes from the language model itself versus explicit runtime structure for planning, state tracking, and reflection.

Method: It introduces a declared reflective runtime protocol that externalizes agent state, confidence, guarded actions, and hypothetical transitions, then evaluates progressively structured agents on Collaborative Battleship.

Results: Explicit world-model planning substantially improves over a greedy baseline, while sparse LLM-based revision adds only marginal gains compared with structured components.

Conclusion: A large share of apparent agent competence may come from explicit runtime design rather than unconstrained LLM reflection, which is useful for building more inspectable agents.

2. Reason in Chains, Learn in Trees: Self-Rectification and Grafting for Multi-turn Agent Policy Optimization

arXiv: 2604.07165 2026-04-08

Problem: Multi-turn agent RL suffers from sparse rewards and poor credit assignment because whole trajectories are often treated as flat, independent samples.

Method: T-STAR merges similar trajectories into a Cognitive Tree, performs introspective valuation for step-level advantage estimation, and uses thought grafting to synthesize corrective reasoning from branch divergences.

Results: The framework recovers more informative learning signals at critical steps and reports consistent gains across hard multi-step reasoning settings.

Conclusion: Tree-structured credit assignment looks like a promising recipe for training agent policies that must reason and self-correct over long horizons.

3. EVGeoQA: Benchmarking LLMs on Dynamic, Multi-Objective Geo-Spatial Exploration

arXiv: 2604.07070 2026-04-08

Problem: Static geo-spatial QA benchmarks miss the real planning difficulty of dynamic user location, charging constraints, and multiple simultaneous objectives.

Method: The authors build EVGeoQA around EV-charging scenarios and evaluate models with GeoRover, a tool-augmented agent framework for dynamic geo-spatial exploration.

Results: Current LLMs can use tools for local subproblems but remain weak at long-range exploration and compound planning under changing context.

Conclusion: Geo-spatial intelligence remains a genuine open challenge for agentic LLMs, especially when multiple real-world constraints interact.

4. Planning Task Shielding: Detecting and Repairing Flaws in Planning Tasks through Turning them Unsolvable

arXiv: 2604.07042 2026-04-08

Problem: Some planning settings need the system to prevent unsafe or flawed states entirely, not just find a path to a goal.

Method: The paper formalizes planning task shielding and proposes an optimal algorithm, allmin, that minimally modifies actions so flawed states become unreachable by rendering the bad task unsolvable.

Results: Experiments show the approach can effectively shield planning tasks of increasing size while preserving minimal change.

Conclusion: This is a neat safety-oriented planning idea: encode forbidden behaviors structurally, instead of relying only on downstream policy filtering.

5. A-MBER: Affective Memory Benchmark for Emotion Recognition

arXiv: 2604.07017 2026-04-08

Problem: Existing emotion benchmarks focus on local signals, while long-term memory benchmarks focus on facts rather than affect, leaving emotional continuity under-evaluated.

Method: A-MBER tests whether a model can infer present emotional state from remembered multi-session interaction history, retrieve supporting evidence, and justify its judgment.

Results: The benchmark is designed to stress long-horizon affect understanding and distinguishes between shallow local inference and real memory-grounded interpretation.

Conclusion: For companion agents and assistants, affective memory is becoming a first-class capability to evaluate rather than a soft, anecdotal nice-to-have.

6. CAFP: A Post-Processing Framework for Group Fairness via Counterfactual Model Averaging

arXiv: 2604.07009 2026-04-08

Problem: Fairness interventions often require retraining models or direct access to sensitive attributes during training, which is unrealistic in many deployed systems.

Method: CAFP is a model-agnostic post-processing method that flips sensitive attributes in counterfactual inputs and averages predictions across factual and counterfactual variants.

Results: The paper argues this removes direct dependence on protected attributes, reduces mutual information with them, and bounds distortion relative to the base model.

Conclusion: Counterfactual post-processing could be an appealing enterprise path for fairness remediation when the original model is a black box.

7. EmoMAS: Emotion-Aware Multi-Agent System for High-Stakes Edge-Deployable Negotiation with Bayesian Orchestration

arXiv: 2604.07003 2026-04-08

Problem: Small models are attractive for private, edge-side negotiation agents, but they struggle with emotional nuance in high-stakes settings.

Method: EmoMAS uses a Bayesian orchestrator to coordinate game-theoretic, reinforcement-learning, and psychological-coherence agents, adapting strategy online without pretraining.

Results: Across negotiation benchmarks in debt, healthcare, emergency response, and education, the orchestrated setup outperforms baseline SLM and LLM approaches.

Conclusion: Specialized multi-agent decomposition may be a stronger route than just scaling one model when negotiation requires both emotional strategy and on-device efficiency.

8. What's Missing in Screen-to-Action? Towards a UI-in-the-Loop Paradigm for Multimodal GUI Reasoning

arXiv: 2604.06995 2026-04-08

Problem: Direct screen-to-action policies for GUI agents are brittle because they under-model UI elements and offer little interpretability.

Method: UILoop reframes GUI reasoning as a cyclic Screen-UI elements-Action process, and the paper adds a 26K-sample UI Comprehension benchmark to evaluate element-level understanding.

Results: The method reports state-of-the-art UI understanding and stronger downstream GUI reasoning performance.

Conclusion: The big takeaway is that GUI agents need explicit UI grounding, not just general multimodal pattern matching over screenshots.

9. Explaining Neural Networks in Preference Learning: a Post-hoc Inductive Logic Programming Approach

arXiv: 2604.06838 2026-04-08

Problem: Preference-learning neural networks are hard to interpret, especially as feature spaces grow and users need understandable rationales.

Method: The authors use ILASP to approximate neural preference models with weak constraints, plus PCA-based preprocessing to keep the explanation process tractable and transparent.

Results: Experiments show the logic-program approach can approximate learned preference behavior both globally and locally while controlling computational cost.

Conclusion: This is a nice example of symbolic approximation as a practical bridge from black-box preference models to human-readable explanations.

10. Beyond Surface Judgments: Human-Grounded Risk Evaluation of LLM-Generated Disinformation

arXiv: 2604.06820 2026-04-08

Problem: LLM judges are increasingly used to rate disinformation risk, but it is unclear whether their evaluations actually track how human readers respond.

Method: The study audits eight frontier LLM judges against 2,043 paired human ratings on 290 aligned articles, framing the question as a proxy-validity problem.

Results: Judges are systematically harsher than humans, recover human ranking only weakly, and rely on different textual cues, agreeing more with one another than with readers.

Conclusion: If you're building automated safety evaluation, judge-model consensus is not the same thing as human-grounded validity.
