Problem: The paper studies computer-use agents whose behavior is documented as explicit SKILL.md files, then asks whether those skills can be mined automatically from GUI trajectories instead of written by hand. The hard part is that interpretable segment clusters are not the same thing as useful control primitives: boundary detection, representation of long interaction segments, and transfer from source workflows to new websites all break easily.

Method: The pipeline has three stages: segment raw GUI trajectories into candidate skill spans, cluster those spans into reusable skill types, then use the mined annotations to train a skill-aware policy. The study evaluates cluster purity against InteraSkill Workflows labels and then applies GRPO-based policy training to test whether the induced skill structure actually improves downstream execution.

Results: On the source benchmark, 5 of 8 discovered clusters reach at least 0.95 purity against InteraSkill Workflows labels, so the mined skills are reasonably readable. But policy gains are weak: GRPO lifts IW skill-step accuracy only from 18.5% to 20.5%, leaves BrowseComp+ nearly unchanged, and even trails simple frequency priors on some source-domain metrics.

Conclusion: This is more valuable as a diagnostic paper than as a finished recipe for agent improvement. It shows that trajectory mining can surface inspectable skill structure, but current segmentation, orderless segment embeddings, and offline reward modeling are not yet enough to deliver robust cross-domain computer-use agents.

Problem: Many agent skills are shipped as long Markdown instructions, which are readable for humans but expensive and indirect for frozen language models to use at inference time. The paper targets the gap between interpretable skill documents and compact behavioral adaptation, especially when long textual skills must be repeatedly retranslated into token-level behavior.

Method: SoftSkill converts a natural-language skill into a short continuous prefix that acts as a latent behavioral prior for a frozen backbone model. The method keeps the base model fixed, trains only a soft skill delta with next-token prediction, and injects a tiny virtual-token prefix at inference instead of hundreds or thousands of Markdown tokens; the paper also tests a sparse trajectory-imitation variant for harder agentic execution.

Results: In the main single-round setting on Qwen3.5-4B, a length-32 SoftSkill prefix improves over no-skill prompting by 8.3 points on SearchQA, 42.1 points on LiveMath, and 1.3 points on DocVQA. Against SkillOpt, it gains 5.2 points on SearchQA and 12.5 points on LiveMath while using only a handful of virtual tokens; the agentic long-horizon setting is harder and shows useful signal but not yet robust procedural compression.

Conclusion: The paper makes a strong case that some reusable skills should be compiled into compact latent controls instead of left as verbose prompt text. Method: The authors adapt DAAM-style cross-attention attribution to speech diffusion models and apply it to CapSpeech-TTS. Problem: Zero-shot flow-matching TTS systems sound strong out of the box, but once deployed they keep mispronouncing out-of-vocabulary names unless the whole model is retrained. That is especially painful for multilingual products, where user names and domain-specific terms appear continuously and need fast correction without harming general speech quality.

Method: FlowEdit keeps the TTS backbone frozen and learns each pronunciation fix as a token-level perturbation in text-embedding space instead of changing model weights. Each correction is stored in a Modern Hopfield Network as content-addressable episodic memory, and at inference time the system retrieves relevant edits through soft attention plus a similarity gate so it can reuse prior fixes for morphologically related names.

Results: On a benchmark of 312 multilingual proper nouns spanning 18 language families, FlowEdit cuts target-word phoneme error rate by 92.7% relative to the zero-shot baseline while preserving general-speech quality. Each correction takes about 15 seconds on a single GPU, which is a practical latency for post-deployment adaptation instead of full retraining.

Conclusion: This is one of the more obviously deployable papers in the batch. It shows a clean pattern for lifelong adaptation in generative speech systems: keep the core model frozen, store small reusable edits in memory, and fix the long tail of real-world pronunciations cheaply after launch.
