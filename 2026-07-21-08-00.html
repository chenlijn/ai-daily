Problem: Scientific Reasoning Graph Extraction tries to turn a paper into an explicit chain of observations, evidence, intermediate claims, and conclusions. Raw LLM outputs often produce malformed graph structure, wrong edge semantics, weak evidence anchors, and invalid roots, which makes them hard to audit in research-agent pipelines.

Method: PEARL is a training-free repair layer rather than a new base extractor. It first maps noisy LLM output into a closed Peircean graph schema, then applies evidence-grounded judge feedback to repair invalid edge types, inference steps, and terminal roots while preserving a full audit trail of every correction.

Results: On five 70-paper ARCHE archives, PEARL lifts strict validity passes from 0/350 for the plain LLM baseline to 300/350 and raises average REA from 0.339 to 0.906. The gain is not just cosmetic formatting, it materially improves whether extracted reasoning graphs satisfy the benchmark's semantic constraints.

Conclusion: This is useful for AI-scientist and research-agent systems that need inspectable reasoning traces instead of free-form summaries. The key contribution is showing that a lightweight repair layer can make LLM-generated scientific graphs far more auditable without retraining the underlying model.

Problem: Long-term agent memory retrieval is hard because memories are heterogeneous and the correct subset changes with the query. Existing methods often retrieve poorly at the start and adapt inefficiently because they rely too much on immediate feedback instead of reusing past retrieval experience.

Method: EAR splits the problem into Exploratory Reflection and Assimilating Reflection. The first iteratively searches and gathers useful retrieval experiences for each query, while the second replays those experiences from a buffer to train a better global reranker in a more sample-efficient way.

Results: On two long-term dialogue benchmarks, EAR improves retrieval by up to 17.9% over the baseline retriever and remains robust under noisy feedback. The reported gain suggests the combination of online exploration plus offline experience reuse is more efficient than one-shot reward-driven retrieval updates.

Conclusion: For production agents, memory quality increasingly depends on the retrieval control loop, not just the storage backend. EAR is interesting because it frames long-term memory as an adaptive learning problem and offers a practical recipe for more reliable recall under limited feedback.

Problem: Chain-of-thought reasoning in vision-language models is accurate but slow and brittle because tokens are generated sequentially and errors accumulate. Diffusion MLLMs are more parallel and revisable, yet their reasoning path control has been underexplored.

Method: ST-Veto is a training-free decoding strategy for diffusion multimodal LLMs. At each diffusion step it vetoes temporally unstable tokens using second-order Taylor prediction of confidence dynamics, filters weakly grounded tokens with image-attention mass, and replaces them with safer candidates to keep generation on a more reliable trajectory.

Results: Across multiple diffusion MLLMs and multimodal reasoning benchmarks, ST-Veto improves accuracy by up to 9% over standard decoding and prior VLM reasoning methods without extra training or added generation cost. The gain is notable because it comes from decoding-time control rather than model retraining.

Conclusion: This is a strong signal that better multimodal reasoning may come from smarter generation control, not only larger models. For teams interested in efficient vision-language systems, decoding-time veto mechanisms look like a promising lever for practical quality gains.
