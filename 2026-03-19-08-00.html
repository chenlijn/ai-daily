Benchmarking Audio-Visual Social Interactivity in Omni Models
arXiv:2603.16859 | Authors: Tianyu Xie et al.
Omni-modal large language models (OLMs) redefine human-machine interaction by natively integrating audio, vision, and text. This paper proposes SocialOmni, a comprehensive benchmark operationalizing evaluation of conversational interactivity across speaker separation, interruption timing control, and natural interruption generation. Benchmarked 12 leading OLMs, uncovering significant variance in social-interaction capabilities.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Existing OLM benchmarks remain anchored to static, accuracy-centric tasks, leaving a gap in assessing social interactivity for natural dialogues.
Method: SocialOmni features 2,000 perception samples and 209 interaction-generation instances with strict temporal and contextual constraints, complemented by controlled audio-visual inconsistency scenarios.
Results: Significant variance across 12 leading OLMs; pronounced decoupling between perceptual accuracy and ability to generate contextually appropriate interruptions.
Conclusion: Understanding-centric metrics alone are insufficient for conversational social competence; diagnostics yield actionable signals for bridging perception-interaction divide.
Internalizing Agency from Reflective Experience
arXiv:2603.16843 | Submitted to ICML 2026
Prevailing outcome-driven post-training methods primarily optimize final success signals, leaving rich environment feedback underutilized. The paper proposes LEAFE (Learning Feedback-Grounded Agency from Reflective Experience), a framework that internalizes recovery agency from reflective experience by summarizing environment feedback into actionable experience and exploring alternative branches.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Outcome-driven methods lead to distribution sharpening - the policy becomes better at reproducing narrow successful behaviors but fails to expand problem-solving capacity (Pass@k) in long-horizon settings.
Method: During exploration, the agent summarizes environment feedback into actionable experience, backtracks to earlier decision points, and explores alternative branches with revised actions, then distills through SFT.
Results: Consistently improves Pass@1 over base model; achieves higher Pass@k than outcome-driven baselines (GRPO) and experience-based methods, with gains up to 14% on Pass@128.
Conclusion: Rich environment feedback can be effectively internalized to build more capable autonomous agents.
Learning to Present: Inverse Specification Rewards for Agentic Slide Generation
arXiv:2603.16839 | Code and Dataset Available
Proposes an OpenEnv-compatible RL environment where LLM agents learn to research topics, plan content, and generate professional HTML slide presentations through tool use. Introduces inverse specification reward that measures how faithfully generated slides convey their intended purpose. Fine-tuned Qwen2.5-Coder-7B via GRPO achieves 91.2% of Claude Opus 4.6's quality.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Automated presentation generation requires coherent content creation, visual design, and audience-aware communication.
Method: Multi-component reward system combining structural validation, render quality assessment, LLM-based aesthetic scoring, content quality metrics, and inverse specification reward. Trained only 0.5% of parameters.
Results: Fine-tuned 7B model achieves 91.2% of Claude Opus 4.6's quality while improving 33.1% over base model. Instruction adherence and tool-use compliance determine agentic task performance more than parameter count.
Conclusion: Open-source SlideRL dataset of 288 multi-turn rollout trajectories released for community use.

Is Conformal Factuality for RAG-based LLMs Robust?
arXiv:2603.16817 | 56 pages
Systematically analyzes reliability and usefulness of conformal factuality for RAG-based LLMs across generation, scoring, calibration, robustness, and efficiency. Proposes novel informativeness-aware metrics. Finds that conformal filtering suffers from low usefulness at high factuality levels and is not robust to distribution shifts.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: While RAG grounds responses in retrieved evidence, it provides no statistical guarantee of correctness. Conformal factuality filtering offers distribution-free reliability but may produce vacuous outputs.
Method: Evaluation across three benchmarks and multiple model families with novel informativeness-aware metrics reflecting task utility.
Results: (i) Low usefulness at high factuality due to vacuous outputs, (ii) Conformal guarantee not robust to distribution shifts, (iii) Lightweight entailment-based verifiers match LLM-based scorers with 100x fewer FLOPs.
Conclusion: Exposes factuality-informativeness trade-offs and fragility under distribution shifts, providing guidance for building reliable RAG pipelines.
Anticipatory Planning for Multimodal AI Agents
arXiv:2603.16777 | Published at CVPR 2026 Findings Track
Most existing multimodal systems remain reactive, optimizing actions in isolation without reasoning about future states. Introduces TraceR1, a two-stage RL framework that explicitly trains anticipatory reasoning by forecasting short-horizon trajectories before execution. Achieves substantial improvements across seven benchmarks covering computer-use and multimodal tool-use reasoning.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Reactive systems lack planning coherence and cannot reliably solve high-level, multi-step tasks requiring long-term goal reasoning.
Method: Stage 1: Trajectory-level RL with rewards enforcing global consistency. Stage 2: Grounded RL fine-tuning using execution feedback from frozen tool agents.
Results: Substantial improvements in planning stability, execution robustness, and generalization over reactive and single-stage baselines across seven benchmarks.
Conclusion: Anticipatory trajectory reasoning is a key principle for building multimodal agents in complex real-world environments.

arXiv:2603.16659 | Economics & AI
Scientific taste—the ability to judge which untested ideas deserve pursuit—governs scientific advance but has never been successfully automated. This paper shows that fine-tuning language models on journal publication decisions recovers evaluative judgment inaccessible to both frontier models and human expertise. Best model achieves 59% accuracy vs. 42% for expert panels and 31% for frontier models.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Capacity that most governs scientific advance is not reasoning but taste, exercised daily by editors and funders but never successfully articulated, taught, or automated.
Method: Fine-tuning on years of publication records from management and economics journals, tested on held-out benchmark of research pitches spanning four quality tiers.
Results: Fine-tuned models surpass every frontier model and expert panel (59% vs 42% vs 31%), with calibrated confidence reaching 100% on highest-confidence predictions.
Conclusion: Scientific taste was deposited in institutional records, waiting to be extracted—providing a scalable mechanism to triage expanding scientific production.
Nonstandard Errors in AI Agents
arXiv:2603.16744 | 45 pages
Deploying 150 autonomous Claude Code agents to independently test six hypotheses about market quality trends, finds that AI agents exhibit sizable nonstandard errors (NSEs)—uncertainty from agent-to-agent variation in analytical choices. Different model families exhibit stable "empirical styles." AI peer review has minimal effect, but exposure to top-rated exemplars reduces interquartile range by 80-99%.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: AI agents may produce different empirical results given the same data and research question, analogous to variation among human researchers.
Method: 150 autonomous Claude Code agents test hypotheses on NYSE TAQ data; three-stage feedback protocol examining peer review and exemplar effects.
Results: AI agents diverge substantially on measure choice; written critiques have minimal effect but top-rated exemplars dramatically reduce dispersion.
Conclusion: Convergence reflects imitation rather than understanding—critical implications for automated policy evaluation and empirical research.

📊 Full Analysis

Results: (1) Models display strategic realism beyond surface rhetoric, (2) Capability uneven across domains, (3) Narratives evolve from containment expectations to systemic entrenchment accounts.
Conclusion: Serves as archival snapshot of model reasoning during unfolding crisis, enabling future studies without hindsight bias.
Are Large Language Models Truly Smarter Than Humans?
arXiv:2603.16197 | 15 pages, 7 tables
Three complementary experiments forming rigorous multi-method contamination audit of six frontier LLMs: GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3, Llama-3.3-70B, and Qwen3-235B. Overall contamination rate of 13.8% (18.1% in STEM, up to 66.7% in Philosophy). Accuracy drops 7.0 percentage points under indirect reference, rising to 19.8 pp in Law and Ethics.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: Most benchmarks are fully public with questions widely mirrored across internet, creating systematic risk that models were trained on evaluation data.
Method: Experiment 1: Lexical contamination detection on 513 MMLU questions. Experiment 2: Paraphrase and indirect-reference diagnostic. Experiment 3: TS-Guessing behavioral probes.
Results: DeepSeek-R1 displays distributed memorization signature (76.6% partial reconstruction, 0% verbatim recall). Contamination ranking: STEM > Professional > Social Sciences > Humanities.
Conclusion: Public leaderboards may systematically overestimate true LLM capabilities due to benchmark contamination.
V-DyKnow: A Dynamic Benchmark for Time-Sensitive Knowledge in Vision Language Models
arXiv:2603.16581 | Dataset and Code Released
Vision-Language Models treat factual knowledge as time-invariant, but real-world facts are time-sensitive. V-DyKnow evaluates time-sensitive factual knowledge in VLMs, analyzing reliability across modalities and efficacy of knowledge editing and multi-modal RAG. VLMs frequently output outdated facts; factual reliability degrades from textual to visual stimuli.

📊 Full Analysis

Problem: VLMs trained on data snapshots implicitly treat factual knowledge as time-invariant, causing outdated predictions.
Method: Dynamic benchmark evaluating time-sensitive factual knowledge across closed- and open-source VLMs, testing knowledge editing and multi-modal RAG methods.
Results: VLMs output outdated facts reflecting training snapshots; alignment approaches fail to consistently update knowledge across modalities.
Conclusion: Highlights fundamental limitations in how VLMs acquire and update time-sensitive knowledge across modalities.
