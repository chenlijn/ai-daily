Problem: RLVR can make LLMs much stronger at verifiable reasoning tasks like competition math, but it often produces chains of thought that weaker models and humans cannot easily follow. The paper targets this compatibility gap, asking how to keep the reasoning gains of RLVR while making the resulting trajectories easier to hand off, inspect, and reuse in mixed-strength systems.

Method: The authors extend tandem training into the RLVR setting. A stronger senior model and a frozen weaker junior stochastically alternate while generating the same reasoning trace, the rollout is scored with a verifiable reward, and the senior is updated with a standard GRPO objective. This forces the senior to solve problems in a way that remains legible and recoverable for the junior instead of drifting into private reasoning habits.

Results: On competition-math training with Qwen3-4B-Instruct, TRL matches vanilla GRPO on the senior model's solo reasoning performance while improving handoff robustness, reducing distribution drift from the junior, and making the generated chain of thought more readable. The abstract does not expose a full benchmark table, but it clearly reports that the compatibility gains come without sacrificing the main RLVR task capability.

Conclusion: This is a useful post-training direction for multi-agent and human-in-the-loop reasoning systems, where readability and recoverability matter as much as raw accuracy. It suggests RLVR pipelines do not have to trade off capability against communicability, which is important for deployable reasoning stacks rather than leaderboard-only systems.

Problem: Small agent models need both dense supervision and reward-driven exploration on long interactive tasks, but the two dominant approaches fail in opposite ways. On-policy distillation learns quickly then plateaus near teacher quality, while RL can in principle exceed the teacher but wastes data early because rewards are sparse and delayed.

Method: ATOD combines the two regimes with an annealed training schedule: OPD dominates early to copy strong teacher behavior, then RL is progressively strengthened to push beyond imitation. The paper also adds Turn-level Disagreement-Uncertainty Reweighting, which highlights high-value turns inside long trajectories so dense supervision is spent where it helps most.

Results: On ALFWorld, WebShop, and Search-QA, ATOD consistently beats competing post-training baselines across three student sizes. The abstract reports a 3.03-point average success-rate gain over OPD, a 23.62-point gain over GRPO, and a 2.16-point advantage over the corresponding teacher models, which is a strong result for efficient agent training.

Conclusion: This is a practical recipe for turning smaller models into stronger autonomous agents without paying the full cost of pure RL from scratch. For production agent stacks, the paper points toward mixed imitation-plus-reward curricula as a more reliable path than choosing distillation or RL alone.

Problem: Language agents that use LLM-based world models can plan flexibly, but they often hallucinate state transitions, and those hallucinations compound over multiple steps. Purely parameterized transition models are easier to score and calibrate, but they are usually weaker planners by themselves, so the paper targets the gap between flexibility and groundedness.

Method: Grounded Iterative Language Planning pairs a small parameterized world-model backbone with an API-based LLM planner. The backbone predicts valid actions, state deltas, risk, and value; the LLM proposes an action and imagined next-state delta; and a consistency gate forces revision when the two disagree, preventing unsupported imagined state changes from propagating.

Results: On real GPT-4o-mini calls, GILP cuts hallucinated-state rate from 0.176 to 0.035. In simulator ablations, it lifts success rate from 0.668 to 0.838 with only about 22% more LLM calls, which is a strong efficiency-quality tradeoff for practical agent planning.

Conclusion: This is one of today's most actionable agent papers because it shows a concrete way to reduce hallucination propagation without replacing the LLM planner entirely. For engineering teams, the pattern of pairing a cheap learned verifier with a stronger language planner looks very deployable.
