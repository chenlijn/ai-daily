1. The Spike, the Sparse and the Sink: Anatomy of Massive Activations and Attention Sinks

https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05498

Problem: Understanding two recurring phenomena in Transformer language models - massive activations (extreme outliers in token channels) and attention sinks (tokens attracting disproportionate attention) - and their functional relationship.

Method: Systematic experiments to study co-occurrence patterns and identify that pre-norm configuration enables co-occurrence. Analyzed how massive activations function as global implicit parameters while attention sinks operate locally across heads.

Results: Discovered that the co-occurrence is largely an architectural artifact of modern Transformer design. Massive activations induce near-constant hidden representations persisting across layers, while attention sinks bias individual heads toward short-range dependencies.

Conclusion: The two phenomena serve related but distinct functions and can be decoupled by ablating pre-norm configuration, providing insights for Transformer architecture optimization.

4. Dissociating Direct Access from Inference in AI Introspection

https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05414

Problem: Understanding the mechanism of introspection in AI models - whether they use direct access to internal states or inference from probability patterns.

Method: Replicated thought injection detection paradigm across large open-source models. Identified two separable mechanisms: probability-matching (inferring from perceived anomaly) and direct access to internal states.

Conclusion: The content-agnostic introspective mechanism aligns with leading theories in philosophy and psychology, advancing our understanding of AI self-awareness capabilities.
