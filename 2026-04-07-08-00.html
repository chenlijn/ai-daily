1. User Turn Generation as a Probe of Interaction Awareness in Language Models

arXiv:2604.02315 · cs.AI · Revised Apr 3, 2026
Sarath Shekkizhar et al.
Standard LLM benchmarks evaluate only the assistant turn, leaving unexplored whether LLMs encode awareness of what follows their responses. This paper proposes user-turn generation as a probe: given a conversation context, generate under the user role. Experiments across 11 open-weight LLMs and 5 datasets reveal that interaction awareness is decoupled from task accuracy, with deterministic generation showing near-zero follow-up rates even at 397B parameters.
ProblemCurrent benchmarks measure only task accuracy, missing whether LLMs understand conversational follow-through and consequence awareness.
MethodUser-turn generation probe: generate user-role responses from model weights; measure grounded follow-up rates under deterministic and stochastic sampling.
ResultsGSM8K scales 41% to 96.8% with size, yet follow-up rates remain near-zero deterministically, reaching 22% only at higher temperature.
ConclusionInteraction awareness is latent and decoupled from task accuracy; collaboration-oriented post-training can increase genuine follow-up behavior.

2. Novel Memory Forgetting Techniques for Autonomous AI Agents: Balancing Relevance and Efficiency

arXiv:2604.02280 · cs.AI, cs.CV · Apr 2, 2026
Sunil Tiwari
Long-horizon conversational agents suffer from uncontrolled memory accumulation causing temporal decay and false memory propagation. This work introduces an adaptive budgeted forgetting framework with relevance-guided scoring (recency, frequency, semantic alignment), achieving long-horizon F1 beyond 0.583 baseline, higher retention consistency, and reduced false memory behavior without increasing context usage.
ProblemAutonomous agents accumulate unbounded memory over long conversations, degrading via false memories and temporal decay (LOCCO drops from 0.455 to 0.05).
MethodAdaptive budgeted forgetting: relevance-guided scoring (recency + frequency + semantic alignment) with bounded optimization to regulate memory.
ResultsLong-horizon F1 exceeds 0.583 baseline; false memory rate reduced; retention consistency improved — all without additional context overhead.
ConclusionStructured forgetting preserves reasoning performance while preventing unbounded memory growth in extended conversational settings.

4. De Jure: Iterative LLM Self-Refinement for Structured Extraction of Regulatory Rules

arXiv:2604.02276 · cs.AI, cs.CL, cs.LG · Apr 2, 2026
Lovedeep Gondara
De Jure is a fully automated, domain-agnostic pipeline for extracting structured regulatory rules from dense legal documents without human annotation. Four stages: normalization to Markdown, LLM-driven semantic decomposition, 19-dimension LLM-as-judge evaluation, and bounded iterative repair. Achieves 73.8% preference over prior work in downstream RAG QA at single-rule depth, 84.0% under broader retrieval.
ProblemConverting hierarchically structured legal text into machine-readable rules is costly, expert-intensive, and requires scarce annotated gold data.
Method4-stage pipeline: document normalization, semantic decomposition, 19-dimension LLM-as-judge evaluation, bounded iterative repair of low-scoring extractions.
ResultsMonotonic quality improvement on finance; generalizes to healthcare and AI governance; 73.8% preference over prior RAG work at single-rule depth.
ConclusionInterpretable evaluation criteria can substitute for human annotation in regulatory domains, enabling scalable regulation-grounded LLM alignment.

5. Do Emotions in Prompts Matter? Effects of Emotional Framing on Large Language Models

arXiv:2604.02236 · cs.AI · Apr 2, 2026
Minda Zhao et al.
This paper examines how first-person emotional framing in user queries affects LLM performance across 6 benchmark domains. Static emotional prefixes cause only small accuracy changes, but effects are more variable in socially grounded tasks. EmotionRL, an adaptive emotional prompting framework, selects emotional framing per query and yields more reliable gains than any fixed emotional prompt.
ProblemEmotional tone is pervasive in human communication, but its influence on LLM behavior is unclear — neither dominant driver nor irrelevant noise has been established.
MethodSystematic evaluation across 6 domains; comparison of static emotional prefixes vs. adaptive selection (EmotionRL) per query.
ResultsStatic prefixes cause only mild accuracy perturbations; more variable in socially grounded tasks; adaptive selection yields more reliable gains than fixed emotion.
ConclusionEmotional tone is a weak, input-dependent signal exploitable through adaptive control but not as a reliable general-purpose intervention.

6. Answering the Wrong Question: Reasoning Trace Inversion for Abstention in LLMs

arXiv:2604.02230 · cs.AI · Apr 2, 2026
Abinitha Gourabathina et al.
Reasoning models excel at complex tasks but have poor abstention abilities. This paper reframes hallucinations causing failed abstention as models "answering the wrong question." Trace Inversion reconstructs the most likely query from a model's reasoning trace and compares it to the original — low similarity flags incorrect answers for abstention. Achieves SOTA abstention across 4 frontier LLMs on 9 datasets, beating baselines in 33/36 settings.
ProblemReasoning models have worse abstention abilities than standard LLMs, often confidently answering questions they should decline.
MethodQuery Misalignment Framework: generate model's reasoning trace; reconstruct likely original query; compare similarity — low similarity flags incorrect answers.
ResultsTrace Inversion boosts abstention across 4 frontier LLMs on 9 QA datasets, beating baselines in 33 out of 36 settings.
ConclusionReframing hallucinations as query misalignment enables more effective abstention; Trace Inversion is a new class of SOTA abstention methods.
Reasoning ModelsAbstentionHallucinationLLM Safety

7. Adaptive Safety through Knowledge (ASK): Uncertainty-Gated Language Assistance for Reinforcement Learning

arXiv:2604.02226 · cs.AI, cs.LG · Apr 2, 2026 — IJCNN 2026
Nathan Gavenski et al.
RL agents struggle with out-of-distribution scenarios due to high uncertainty and random behavior. ASK combines smaller LMs with trained RL policies, using Monte Carlo Dropout to assess uncertainty and querying the LM only when uncertainty exceeds a threshold. This selective approach preserves policy efficiency while leveraging LM reasoning in uncertain situations, achieving 0.95 reward on FrozenLake transfer tasks.
ProblemRL agents exhibit high uncertainty in OOD scenarios; large LMs are computationally expensive; smaller ones lack autonomous planning capability.
MethodASK: Monte Carlo Dropout estimates policy uncertainty; LM queried only when uncertainty exceeds threshold — selective neuro-symbolic integration.
ResultsNo in-domain improvement but robust OOD transfer navigation on FrozenLake achieving 0.95 reward without full retraining.
ConclusionEffective neuro-symbolic integration requires careful orchestration and sufficient model scale; simple combination is insufficient for OOD generalization.

ProblemAttention visualization is architecture-specific and requires backpropagation, effectively doubling GPU memory and limiting model-agnostic applicability.
MethodPerturbation-based token removal with three complementary matrices (Angular, Magnitude, Dimensional) combined into a composite importance score.
