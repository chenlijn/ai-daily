1. Four-Axis Decision Alignment for Long-Horizon Enterprise AI Agents

arXiv: 2604.19457 2026-04-23 Heat Score: 88 (Very High) HF Trending: No | HF Upvotes: 0

Why it matters now: This is the strongest enterprise-agent paper of the day because it ties long-horizon agent evaluation to regulated decision workflows like underwriting and claims, which is exactly where real deployment pressure sits. It also resonates with today's broader agent conversation on HN and GitHub, where reliability, auditability, and workflow control matter more than raw one-shot accuracy.

Problem: The paper targets a gap that shows up immediately in regulated deployments: a long-horizon agent can appear successful on an aggregate task score while still hallucinating facts, breaking compliance rules, or making overconfident decisions. In domains like loan qualification and insurance claims, those failure modes are materially different, but existing evaluation often collapses them into one scalar and hides what actually went wrong.

Method: The authors decompose decision alignment into four separately measurable axes: factual precision, reasoning coherence, compliance reconstruction, and calibrated abstention. They then build a controlled benchmark, LongHorizon-Bench, around loan qualification and claims adjudication, and run six memory architectures through the same setup so the evaluation can reveal which design choices help or hurt each axis rather than only reporting overall success.

Results: On LongHorizon-Bench, the decomposition exposed patterns that aggregate accuracy would have missed. Retrieval-heavy memory setups fell apart on factual precision, schema-anchored designs paid a scaffolding cost, and simple summarization with a fact-preservation prompt was a surprisingly strong baseline across several axes. All six architectures still committed on every case, showing that calibrated abstention remains a major unsolved problem. The paper emphasizes directional findings more than a large scoreboard of public metrics.

Conclusion: The useful contribution is not another agent benchmark number, but a cleaner measurement framework for enterprise AI governance. For real-world decision systems, the paper argues that regulatory reconstruction and abstention need to be first-class evaluation targets, which is a practical lesson for any team deploying agents into high-stakes business workflows.

Method: AblateCell follows a reproduce-then-ablate loop. Results: The paper reports superior performance over prior methods on industrial datasets including MVTecAD, with the gain attributed to both higher-quality synthetic defect generation and the asymmetric reconstruction design. The extracted text confirms strong benchmark performance, but the visible snippet does not expose every metric value.
