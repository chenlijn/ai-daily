Do Metrics for Counterfactual Explanations Align with User Perception?
arXiv:2603.15607 | Accepted at XAI 2026 | cs.AI, cs.HC
Problem
Current algorithmic metrics for evaluating counterfactual explanations in AI systems lack validation against actual human judgments of explanation quality.
Method
Empirical study comparing algorithmic metrics with human ratings across three datasets, analyzing correlations and predictive combinations.
Results
Correlations between algorithmic metrics and human ratings are weak and dataset-dependent; increasing metric count doesn't improve predictions.
Conclusion
Current counterfactual evaluation metrics fail to capture key aspects of explanation quality as perceived by users, requiring more human-centered approaches.

Are Dilemmas and Conflicts in LLM Alignment Solvable? A View from Priority Graph
arXiv:2603.15527 | cs.AI, cs.CY
Problem
LLMs increasingly face conflicts and dilemmas in diverse scenarios, but understanding and resolving these alignment conflicts remains challenging.
Method
Models LLM preferences as a priority graph with context-specific edges, revealing "priority hacking" vulnerability and proposing runtime verification.
Results
The priority graph reveals alignment is challenging because the graph is neither static nor consistent across contexts; many ethical dilemmas are philosophically irreducible.
Conclusion
Unified stable LLM alignment is very challenging; runtime verification can enhance robustness but fundamental ethical dilemmas remain open problems.
Understanding Reasoning in LLMs through Strategic Information Allocation under Uncertainty
arXiv:2603.15500 | cs.AI, cs.LG
Problem
The mechanisms behind LLM "Aha moments" during reasoning (like self-correction following "Wait" tokens) remain unclear.
Method
Information-theoretic framework decomposing reasoning into procedural information and epistemic verbalization of uncertainty for downstream control.
Results
Strong reasoning performance is driven by uncertainty externalization rather than specific surface tokens; purely procedural reasoning can become informationally stagnant.
Conclusion
Epistemic verbalization enables continued information acquisition and is critical for achieving information sufficiency in LLM reasoning.
