Method: Causely adds a causal intelligence layer that converts observability data into a live model of services, dependencies, and causal relationships. Conclusion: The paper is a strong signal for enterprise agent architecture: better world models can beat bigger prompts. For production AI ops, it suggests that ontology-backed state layers may become a core primitive for safe, low-cost incident agents.

arXiv: 2605.18299 2026-05-20 Heat Score: 67 (Medium) HF Trending: No | HF Upvotes: 0

Why it matters now: Search and tool-use reasoning remain hot, but training them usually depends on expensive external supervision. This paper matters now because it proposes a cheaper self-improving route that fits the current post-training wave.

Problem: Search-augmented reasoning agents live or die by the quality of each query, but outcome-level RL gives every search step the same reward. That makes it hard to learn which retrieval decisions were actually useful without relying on a stronger teacher or extra human annotations.

Method: SD-Search uses on-policy hindsight self-distillation, where the same model plays a student role at inference time and a teacher role that can look back at grouped rollouts and outcomes. The student then matches the teacher's query distribution at search positions via token-level Jensen-Shannon divergence, adding dense step-level supervision on top of GRPO.

Results: The abstract positions SD-Search as a training improvement for search-heavy reasoning without extra external supervision, and reports better query decisions and downstream performance than coarse trajectory-reward baselines. However, the currently extracted content does not include the detailed benchmark numbers.

Conclusion: The interesting part is the training recipe: it squeezes better process supervision out of the policy itself instead of paying for bigger teachers. That makes it relevant for teams trying to improve search or tool-use agents without dramatically increasing training complexity or serving cost.
